    Correspondence with the Instructor
    🔗
    Students who email the instructor with a question should expect a response within 24 hours (if you are emailing after 5 pm or on weekends, you should expect a response after 9 am the following business day). Emails should be from a UA email address and include the student’s first and last name, the course they are in, and a clearly stated question. Emails without this minimal information will not receive responses. Emails should maintain a professional tone; emails containing inappropriate material or language will be ignored and, in extreme cases, sent to the Office of Rights, Compliance, and Accountability for further action.
    🔗
    RSI Policy
    🔗
    To be compliant with federal law and Northwest Commission on Colleges and Universities (NWCCU) accreditation requirements, UAF faculty must ensure that all courses in which faculty and students are not physically located in the same space, for which students use federal financial aid, have regular and substantive interaction (RSI) between students and instructors. Regular refers to scheduled, predictable interactions. Substantive refers to engaging students in teaching, learning, and assessment, consistent with course content.
    🔗
    As your faculty member, I have included the following actions in the course to meet RSI requirements:
    • Meeting with each student in the first 3 weeks of the course

    Academic Integrity Policy
    🔗
    As described by UAF, scholastic dishonesty constitutes a violation of the university’s rules and regulations and is punishable in accordance with the procedures outlined by UAF. Academic dishonesty includes but is not limited to cheating on any assignment, plagiarism, and collusion. Cheating includes providing answers to or taking answers from another student or source, using non-allowed materials on an assessment (such as calculators or phones). Plagiarism consists of the use of another author’s words or arguments without attribution. Collusion includes unauthorized collaboration with another person in preparing written work to fulfill any course requirement. Scholastic dishonesty is punishable by a zero on the assignment for the first offense and a second offense removal from the course with a grade of “F.” For more information, go to https://www.alaska.edu/bor/policy/09.02-Student%20Rights%20and%20Responsibilities.pdf
    🔗
    AI Policy
    🔗
    This course requires completion of various assignments that assess understanding and application of the course content. Students are expected to do their own work. Students are not allowed to use any artificial intelligence (AI) tools, such as text generators, paraphrasers, summarizers, mathematical solvers, or mathematical graphing tools, to complete any part of their reflections, discussions, quizzes, or assessments. These tools can help with understanding practice materials. Using AI tools for reflections, discussions, quizzes, or assessments is considered academic dishonesty. It will result in a grade of N for the assignment and may lead to disciplinary action. If you have any questions about what constitutes acceptable use of AI tools, please consult the instructor before submitting the work.
    🔗
    Grade Reporting
    🔗
    UAF has various grade reporting processes to help students stay on track and make informed decisions about course progress toward degree completion. The UAF Early Warning, Midterm, and Withdrawal grade reports are reported in Nanook Navigator and UA Online and are sent out to advisors and observers. In addition, a Grade Progress link has been provided in Canvas for students to monitor their progress. Students will also receive regular progress reports through Airtable so that they can see their progress throughout the semester. It is the student’s responsibility to ask if there are questions about the grade or anything stated in a progress report. These progress reports are also a time to review study habits and identify what may need to be done to improve a grade.
    🔗
    Equity
    🔗
    Mastery grading makes course expectations (assignments and grades) much clearer for students. This can make unstated expectations visible and help instructors identify and remove unnecessary requirements (such as those focused on compliance rather than learning). For the students, mastery grading provides clear expectations of what is needed to earn a particular grade. Outcomes and rubrics are provided in advance so that those expectations are transparent.
    🔗
    Mastery-based grading also allows students to take ownership of their learning. Grades here are based on showing your understanding or on making mistakes and learning from them. Everyone learns at different paces, and mastery learning allows the opportunity to spend more time on areas that you have difficulty with and less time on areas that you know.
